### Why Elasticsearch’s storage footprint is larger [\#](/blog/elasticsearch-log-analytics-clickhouse#why-elasticsearchs-storage-footprint-is-larger)

Elasticsearch’s larger index partly reflects broader search features such as phrase queries, fuzzy matching, wildcard search, and ranking\-related metadata, which typical OTel log analysis queries like those in this benchmark generally do not rely on.

To test how much that matters for storage consumption here, we also ran reduced\-feature Elasticsearch variants.

A [moderate variant](https://github.com/ClickHouse/TextBench/blob/main/elasticsearch/create_index_v2.sh) that disabled norms ([norms parameter set to false](https://www.elastic.co/docs/reference/elasticsearch/mapping-reference/norms)) and reduced the main Body text field to document\-only postings ([index\_options set to docs](https://www.elastic.co/docs/reference/elasticsearch/mapping-reference/index-options)) lowered total footprint by only [about 2%](https://github.com/ClickHouse/TextBench/blob/main/elasticsearch/results/tuning/storage_1B.png).

Larger storage reductions ([\~20% smaller on disk](https://github.com/ClickHouse/TextBench/blob/main/elasticsearch/results/tuning/storage_1B.png), still 4 times larger than ClickHouse) required much more [aggressive disabling](https://github.com/ClickHouse/TextBench/blob/main/elasticsearch/create_index_v3.sh) of indexing ([index parameter set to false](https://www.elastic.co/docs/reference/elasticsearch/mapping-reference/mapping-index)) on additional fields, producing a configuration that is less representative of typical Elasticsearch log deployments (for example, filtering would become much slower on most OTel fields beyond the small subset referenced by our query suite).
