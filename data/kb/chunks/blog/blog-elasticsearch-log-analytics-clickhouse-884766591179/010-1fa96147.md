> Overall, ClickHouse stores the same OTel log data in roughly one\-fifth the space required by Elasticsearch.

This is consistent across all three scales.

- **1B:** ClickHouse **[49\.49 GiB](https://github.com/ClickHouse/TextBench/blob/main/clickhouse/results/storage/es_vs_ch/clickhouse_storage_sizes.jsonl)** vs Elasticsearch **[245\.06 GiB](https://github.com/ClickHouse/TextBench/blob/main/clickhouse/results/storage/es_vs_ch/elasticsearch_storage_sizes.csv)** → **4\.95× less in ClickHouse**
- **10B:** ClickHouse **[496\.84 GiB](https://github.com/ClickHouse/TextBench/blob/main/clickhouse/results/storage/es_vs_ch/clickhouse_storage_sizes.jsonl)** vs Elasticsearch **[2\.40 TiB](https://github.com/ClickHouse/TextBench/blob/main/clickhouse/results/storage/es_vs_ch/elasticsearch_storage_sizes.csv)** → **4\.95× less in ClickHouse**
- **50B:** ClickHouse **[2\.43 TiB](https://github.com/ClickHouse/TextBench/blob/main/clickhouse/results/storage/es_vs_ch/clickhouse_storage_sizes.jsonl)** vs Elasticsearch **[12\.01 TiB](https://github.com/ClickHouse/TextBench/blob/main/clickhouse/results/storage/es_vs_ch/elasticsearch_storage_sizes.csv)** → **4\.95× less in ClickHouse**

### Columnar storage footprint [\#](/blog/elasticsearch-log-analytics-clickhouse#columnar-storage-footprint)

The main column\-oriented storage structures already differ sharply. Elasticsearch’s doc\_values alone take substantially more space than ClickHouse’s column files.

- **1B:** ClickHouse **[39\.33 GiB](https://github.com/ClickHouse/TextBench/blob/main/clickhouse/results/storage/es_vs_ch/clickhouse_storage_sizes.jsonl)** vs Elasticsearch **[102\.49 GiB](https://github.com/ClickHouse/TextBench/blob/main/clickhouse/results/storage/es_vs_ch/elasticsearch_storage_sizes.csv)** → **2\.61× less in ClickHouse**
- **10B:** ClickHouse **[393\.54 GiB](https://github.com/ClickHouse/TextBench/blob/main/clickhouse/results/storage/es_vs_ch/clickhouse_storage_sizes.jsonl)** vs Elasticsearch **[1\.00 TiB](https://github.com/ClickHouse/TextBench/blob/main/clickhouse/results/storage/es_vs_ch/elasticsearch_storage_sizes.csv)** → **2\.61× less in ClickHouse**
- **50B:** ClickHouse **[1\.92 TiB](https://github.com/ClickHouse/TextBench/blob/main/clickhouse/results/storage/es_vs_ch/clickhouse_storage_sizes.jsonl)** vs Elasticsearch **[5\.02 TiB](https://github.com/ClickHouse/TextBench/blob/main/clickhouse/results/storage/es_vs_ch/elasticsearch_storage_sizes.csv)** → **2\.61× less in ClickHouse**

Notably, ClickHouse includes every OTel log field in its column files, whereas `Body`, the largest field in the dataset, is excluded from Elasticsearch’s doc\_values because it is mapped as [text](https://www.elastic.co/docs/reference/elasticsearch/mapping-reference/text) and therefore populates only the inverted index.

### Compression efficiency of ClickHouse [\#](/blog/elasticsearch-log-analytics-clickhouse#compression-efficiency-of-clickhouse)

This already hints at the main reason for the overall storage gap: ClickHouse compresses its column files extremely well.

- **1B:** uncompressed **[642\.42 GiB](https://github.com/ClickHouse/TextBench/blob/main/clickhouse/results/storage/es_vs_ch/clickhouse_storage_sizes.jsonl)** → compressed **[39\.33 GiB](https://github.com/ClickHouse/TextBench/blob/main/clickhouse/results/storage/es_vs_ch/clickhouse_storage_sizes.jsonl)** → **16\.33× compression**
- **10B:** uncompressed **[6\.26 TiB](https://github.com/ClickHouse/TextBench/blob/main/clickhouse/results/storage/es_vs_ch/clickhouse_storage_sizes.jsonl)** → compressed **[393\.54 GiB](https://github.com/ClickHouse/TextBench/blob/main/clickhouse/results/storage/es_vs_ch/clickhouse_storage_sizes.jsonl)** → **16\.29× compression**
- **50B:** uncompressed **[31\.31 TiB](https://github.com/ClickHouse/TextBench/blob/main/clickhouse/results/storage/es_vs_ch/clickhouse_storage_sizes.jsonl)** → compressed **[1\.92 TiB](https://github.com/ClickHouse/TextBench/blob/main/clickhouse/results/storage/es_vs_ch/clickhouse_storage_sizes.jsonl)** → **16\.29× compression**

Note that higher ratios are possible when optimizing data types and on\-disk ordering specifically for compression. In an nginx log example, [ClickHouse reaches up to 178× compression](https://clickhouse.com/blog/log-compression-170x).
