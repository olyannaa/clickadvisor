As a [reminder](/blog/elasticsearch-log-analytics-clickhouse#measuring-hot-runs), unlike the cold runs above, hot runs keep the systems running between executions, so the Linux page cache remains available. For each query, we execute the same query three times in a row, with [query\-result caches](/blog/elasticsearch-log-analytics-clickhouse#caches-considered-in-this-benchmark) disabled in both systems, but with their equivalent [filter\-evaluation caches](/blog/elasticsearch-log-analytics-clickhouse#caches-considered-in-this-benchmark) left enabled. We then take the fastest of the three runs as the hot result.

Each query in the OTel log query suite was measured under these conditions, and the chart above sums the resulting per\-query runtimes.

> **Hot queries matter too in observability.**  
> Dashboards auto\-refresh, investigations rerun similar filters, and interactive drill\-down often revisits the same working set.

With warm caches, the gap narrows, but ClickHouse still completes the full query suite faster than Elasticsearch at every scale.

- **1B:** ClickHouse **[0\.68s](https://github.com/ClickHouse/TextBench/blob/main/clickhouse/results/m6i.8xlarge_1b.json)** vs Elasticsearch **[1\.78s](https://github.com/ClickHouse/TextBench/blob/main/elasticsearch/results/m6i.8xlarge_otel_logs_1b.json)** → **2\.62× faster in ClickHouse**
- **10B:** ClickHouse **[4\.54s](https://github.com/ClickHouse/TextBench/blob/main/clickhouse/results/m6i.8xlarge_10b.json)** vs Elasticsearch **[7\.89s](https://github.com/ClickHouse/TextBench/blob/main/elasticsearch/results/m6i.8xlarge_otel_logs_10b.json)** → **1\.74× faster in ClickHouse**
- **50B:** ClickHouse **[20\.3s](https://github.com/ClickHouse/TextBench/blob/main/clickhouse/results/m6i.8xlarge_50b.json)** vs Elasticsearch **[34\.2s](https://github.com/ClickHouse/TextBench/blob/main/elasticsearch/results/m6i.8xlarge_otel_logs_50b.json)** → **1\.69× faster in ClickHouse**

To understand why, we again break results down by query type.

### Hot runtime by query: caching narrows the gap, but not uniformly [\#](/blog/elasticsearch-log-analytics-clickhouse#hot-runtime-by-query-caching-narrows-the-gap-but-not-uniformly)

With warm caches, some query gaps narrow noticeably, but the per\-query breakdown shows that caching does not eliminate the underlying execution\-model differences between the two systems.

![Blog-FTS-vs-Elasticsearch.006.png](/uploads/Blog_FTS_vs_Elasticsearch_006_ac18f0bab2.png)

> Overall, caching narrows the gap for retrieval\-heavy queries, but not for search\-plus\-analytics workloads, where downstream aggregation still dominates.

- **Q1–Q3 incident drill\-down queries** move closer under hot conditions, which is consistent with both systems benefiting from [cached predicate](/blog/elasticsearch-log-analytics-clickhouse#caches-considered-in-this-benchmark) evaluation.

That said, the [equivalent caches](/blog/elasticsearch-log-analytics-clickhouse#caches-considered-in-this-benchmark) are **not identical in granularity**: Elasticsearch caches which **documents** matched a filter before, while ClickHouse caches filter results at the level of **granules** (row blocks). ClickHouse can therefore skip whole granules quickly, but still needs to identify the concrete matching rows within the surviving granules.

- **Q6–Q9 service breakdown and trend\-analysis queries** still show clear ClickHouse advantages, especially at larger scales, because these queries continue to spend substantial time in downstream aggregation after matching rows have been identified.
